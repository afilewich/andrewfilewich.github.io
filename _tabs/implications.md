## What This Project Suggests

This project suggests that short pre-laboratory videos may be a useful support tool in undergraduate kinesiology laboratory courses, especially for helping students prepare before class, understand procedures more clearly, and feel more confident entering the lab.

## Why the Videos May Have Helped

One likely reason the videos were helpful is that they allowed students to become familiar with the procedures before arriving at the laboratory session. In a practical and time-sensitive environment, this may reduce confusion and allow students to focus more on carrying out the task itself.

Rather than replacing in-person teaching, the videos appear to have supported students by making in-person lab time more efficient and easier to navigate.

## The Most Important Tension in the Findings

A major takeaway from the project was the gap between **positive perceptions** and **low uptake**.

Students who used the videos often described them positively, but overall video use across the course remained low. This means that a learning resource can be useful in principle, yet still have limited impact if students do not consistently engage with it.

## What Instructors Can Learn

For kinesiology instructors, this project suggests that creating a helpful resource is only part of the task. It is also important to:

- make students clearly aware that the resource exists
- integrate the resource into normal course workflow
- ensure the videos align closely with what happens in the laboratory
- consider using videos as preparation tools rather than replacements for in-person instruction

## What I Would Improve in Future Versions

If this project were expanded in the future, I would improve it by:

- creating videos for every laboratory session
- improving alignment between the videos and the exact lab activities
- making the videos more visible within the course structure
- considering ways to encourage stronger uptake, such as pre-lab checkpoints or required preparation tasks
- including objective outcome measures such as lab performance or skill accuracy

## Final Reflection

This project showed that instructional design and implementation need to be considered together. A resource may be well designed and still have limited impact if it is not used regularly.

For kinesiology education, this means that multimedia tools should not be judged only by whether students like them, but also by whether they are clearly integrated into the learning environment in a way that supports consistent use.
